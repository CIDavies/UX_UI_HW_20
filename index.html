Government website redesign

For this project, I worked on redesigning the website’s UX and UI. This included restructuring the site navigation and creating a new, comprehensive style guide.

Problem

The existing CFIA website was not user-friendly, with complex user journeys and an unattractive interface. Research showed that the website was not meeting user needs and needed to be streamlined and simplified.

Solution

I created a new design system for the site, simplified key users journeys and restructured the site navigation to deliver an improved user experience for desktop and mobile users.

My role and responsibilities

I worked with another designer in the discovery phase and then worked independently for the rest of the design process as user researcher, UX and UI designer.

Tools

Figma, InVision, Miro, Google suite.

Research

User flow analysis and heuristic evaluation

To fully understand user pain points, we analysed the existing user journey flows, navigation and heuristics on the CFIA website. We focused on food recalls and food safety information, as these were the most prominent pages on the website and the core function of the CFIA.

A fragmented user flow left users confused and frustrated.

Users struggled to navigate the existing site. Unable to easily return to the CFIA homepage, they frequently ended up on the main Canadian Government website. Complex information architecture and too much content left users scrolling and scanning multiple pages to try and find what they needed.

Usability testing

We created a proto persona to focus our research, then conducted 10 usability tests with both mobile and desktop users. Poor success rates for the tasks we tested highlighted issues with the navigation, styling and UI components. After categorising and prioritising our results, clear pain points emerged.

Analysing test results

After categorising and prioritising our results, clear pain points emerged.

Pain points:

  • CTAs not obvious
  • overwhelming homepage, too much content and poor content chunking
  • breadcrumbs and logo confuse the user, there is no trusted way to return to the homepage
  • information is hard to find, sections are unclear to users
  • navigation, copy and content organisation does not meet users’ expectations
  • content confuses the user and doesn’t allow them to easily achieve their goals.

Ideation

Sitemap

After identifying the key problems with the current navigation, I focused on redesigning the site map. I conducted a card sort to create a new navigation structure and organisation.

Moodboard

Before beginning the design phase, I looked at other websites, including other food agencies and government sites, pulling together a moodboard for layout and UI inspiration.

Prototype

Low-fidelity prototype and navigation

I based my navigation on the ‘category’ LATCH principle. Grouping content into categories was clearest for users. Organising by another principle would make it difficult for a user with no prior knowledge of the CFIA to find the content they need.

Within each category, I ordered the secondary navigation content by hierarchy. The hierarchy was informed by looking at the ‘most requested’ sections on the existing CFIA website.

Rapid User Testing

Rapid user tests

I conducted 10 rapid user tests on my wireframes and then iterated my designs based on the results. Key changes included:

  • adding colour to make content chunking clearer
  • creating a language option component to make the option clearer for users
  • finessing interactions so the mobile search bar closed when a user clicked anywhere else on the page.

Visual Design and Style Guide

With such a wide remit and large amount of content, I knew the visual styling of the CFIA website needed to be clear and consistent for the user. Before developing a high-fidelity prototype, I created a completely new style guide and design system.

Iterations

I applied my design system to my mid-fidelity prototype and iterated the design.

  • I removed the ‘Social channels’ menu and instead added social icons to the footer. This information will also be on the Contact Us page and can be accessed if the user wants to share the page.
  • I added a ‘Share this page’ overlay, which appears in the centre of the screen whenever the ‘Share’ button is pressed. I created social components as opposed to words which are instantly recognisable and interactive. Guidelines for these icons were added to the style guide.

  • I iterated my sitemap, separated the ‘Food’ menu items into two areas and made the navigation menu titles more descriptive.
  • One of the CFIA’s primary functions is safeguarding food and the food economy, therefore I needed to make this content easier for users to find.
  • I split the content between consumers and businesses and included tertiary pages from my site map. Adding tertiary pages in this instance makes it easier for users to find specific content quicker as it avoids the content being buried too deep for them to find.

Testing

I purposely included some of the tasks I asked users to complete in my first round of user testing and in the navigation testing done on the current CFIA website. This helped me measure any improvements I had made to the original design and identify what needed iterating. I conducted 4 desktop and 3 mobile user tests.

As the task scores were high, I focused on the qualitative feedback to figure out where to make improvements. I collated all feedback and ordered it by prominence to highlight the most pressing issues, annotated my design prototypes with relevant feedback and organised the results into a prioritisation matrix.

Iterations

User test feedback highlighted that I needed to:

  • make food recalls and anything health risk-related more obvious
  • add dates to news and recalls
  • make it easier to contact the CFIA.

  • I split up the news and recalls and gave the recalls priority as they always contain information regarding health risks. I added a red ‘recall’ tag to visually highlight them to users.  I added dates to all articles to make it clearer which were most recent. 
  • I standardised the size of the read more buttons to keep the visual design consistent. 
  • The new layout maintains the popular alternating coloured sections and images, which users particularly liked. It also maintained lots of white space, which means the design can accommodate more content when required.

  • Added ‘Contact Us’ link at bottom of the mobile menu, which is where users looked for it.
  • Added it to the global navigation on the desktop prototype.
  • Pinned the mobile navigation so key functions like search, chat and menu were alway easy for the user to access.
  • Pinned the Live chat button on the desktop so that users could easily access it.
  • Changed the mobile global navigation layout to make it clearer and make better use of the space.

Final thoughts

When starting on the re-design, designing mobile-first was crucial. The CFIA mandate is broad and they have a lot of content on their site, so breaking this down in a way that was simple for users to view and interrogate was very important. Designing on a mobile screen first focused my mind on content hierarchy and streamlining the user journey.

User testing highlighted the contradictory journeys and preferences of users and showed how important it was for a government website to work for all users. This was especially important for navigation to key pages such as contact pages or food safety information.

Future considerations

I would continue to test and iterate my existing designs, particularly the food recall page, to see if the design works for users and allows them to easily understand the information.

It would also be useful to engage with stakeholders at the CFIA to see what is important from their perspective and where improved website functionality or content could help them achieve business goals or streamline their operations.
